New York City
Nightlife:
The Price of Popularity

Behind the scenes

of your nightclub experience is a billion-dollar, highly-competitive industry. To stay in the game, the masterminds behind nightclubs shell out hundreds of thousands of dollars per night to keep their venue a desireable destination in the eyes of party-goers. In New York City, the price of popularity is high.

Spending Money to Make Money

Expenses Per Year

Marquee nightclub at 10th Avenue and 25th St. — a popular destination — has about $11 million in annual expenses, compared to about $15 million in annual revenue.

Money is made

by attracting patrons to the club that its owners know will spend big. To accomplish that, great care — and money — goes into producing an atmosphere the client will be attracted to. As you'll find out, that means a nightclub experience is lived very differently depending on who you are, how much money you can spend, and even what gender or social status you belong to.

Promoter

10:00 p.m.

Going to Dinner

Promoters are hired by clubs to bring in "scene setters" — people that give cache to a venue just by showing up. Promoters are in charge of forming a crew of beautiful women, models, celebrities and other patrons that will add to the atmosphere. Club owners deem these scene setters so valuable, they are willing to offer the promoter and his or her crew special perks, including free dinners before a night out.

11:45 p.m.

Getting to the Club

While some patrons rely on tickets or the club's doorman to enter a venue, typically people that arrive with promoters are guaranteed access and skip the line. Promoters are often given a flat rate for working on a given night, and occasionally are given a bonus for each desireable "scene setter" they bring in. Promoters generally aim to bring in 10-20 scene setters per night. They are also expected to arrive at the club at a specific time, which is typically around 11:45 or midnight — before the club gets busy. That way, the scene is already set.

2:00 a.m.

Nightlife and Scene Setting

Promoters are given a table for their crews. To keep it stocked with alcohol, each promoter is given a nightly allowance of bottle and drink tickets. Promoters with a full table can get as many as eight bottles in a given night, and they are permitted to keep left over tickets for other nights. At 2 a.m., the club is in full swing, and promoters have likely ordered a 3rd or 4th round. Club owners expect a promoter's table to stay packed during the night, especially if the promoter's table is near a client.

4:00 a.m.

Closing Time

Most nightclubs close at 4 a.m., when it is no longer legal to serve alcohol in New York City. Promoters are expected to stay at a club until at least one hour before closing time, and perhaps later if they are seated next to a client. They are also expected to keep their crew happy — which may mean paying for rides home or taking the crew to another club. Considering a club has between one and 5 promoters working on a given night, and most clubs are open 6 nights per week, the annual promotional expenses are vast.

Client

12:00 a.m.

Arrive at Venue

A client can show up whenever he or she desires, there's only two caveats. First, there must be an available table for purchase, which may be tricky on popular party nights. Second, the client must be able to afford the table. In most cases, that means spending a great deal of money. Often, a client will call ahead to book a table and will be required to purchase a minimum of three bottles. At club prices, that can run a large tab.

Club-Goer

12:00 a.m.

Getting In

Club-goers are a catch-all term for any other patrons that frequent a club. They don't have connections with the owners, they don't walk in with a promoter, and generally they can't afford a table or bottle service. Club-goers generally pay a cover charge or rely on the doorman's discretion for entry. If a club is crowded on a particular night, a bouncer may require a club-goer to agree to a bar tab minimum.
